Madam CJ Walker, Entrepeneur & Philanthropist

Born Sarah Breedlove

Early Life

Sarah Breedlove - who later takes the name and creates a business empire called Madam C. J. Walker – is born on December 23, 1867 near Delta, Louisiana. She is born on a cotton plantation where her parents, Owen and Minerva Anderson Breedlove, had been enslaved before the end of the Civil War. Breedlove is the youngest of five children and the only one born after the Emancipation Proclamation - therefore, she is born free. In 1873, her mother, Minerva Anderson, dies and in 1875, her father Owen Breedlove dies. Breedlove moves to Vicksburgh, MS to be with her sister, Louvenia.

Marriage, Motherhood and Early Origins of Business

In 1881, at age 14, Breedlove marries Moses McWilliams to escape abuse from her brother-in-law. Her only daughter, Lelia (later known as A’Lelia Walker) is born on June 6, 1885. Breedlove is 18. At 20, in 1887, Breedlove's husband Moses dies. Breedlove moves to St. Louis to join her four brothers, Alexander, James, Solomon, and Owen. Jr., who are working as barbers. In her 20s, Breedlove suffers from scalp ailment that causes her to lose most of her hair. She consults with her brothers for advice and experiments with home-made and store brought products. She starts using products from Annie Malone, and becomes a sales agent for Malone's Poro Co. In 1894, she marries John Davis.

Becomes Madam CJ Walker

In 1905 Breedlove's brothers die, and she separates from 2nd husband John Davis. In July of that same year, at age 38, she moves to Denver, CO as a sales agent for Malone. She marries her 3rd husband, Charles Joseph Walker, and changes her name to Madam CJ Walker. She begins selling Madam Walker's Wonderul Hair Grower, a scalp conditioning and healing formula, which she says came to her in a dream. Madam Walker’s five original products were Madam Walker’s Wonderful Hair Grower, Temple Salve, Tetter Salve, Vegetable Shampoo and Glossine. Starting in September 1906, Breedloves travels for a year and a half through the South and Southeast, selling her products door to door, doing demonstrations in churches and lodges, and devising her sales and marketing strategies. In 1908 at age 41, she moves to Pittsburgh where she opens Lelia College, to train Walker "hair culturists." In 1910 she divorces Charles Joseph Walker.

Later Life

In 1910 at age 43, Breedlove settles in Indianapolis, Indiana, where she builds a factory, a hair and manicure salon and another training school. Less than a year later, she makes headlines in the black press for $1,000 contribution to the building fund of the "colored" Indianapolis Senate Avenue YMCA. In 1913, she travels to Central America and Caribbean to expand her business. In that same year, her daughter A'Lelia moves into Harlem townhouse and Walker Salon, designed by the black architect Vertner Tandy. In 1916, at 49, Breedlove moves to NYC, leaving daily operations of the Madam CJ Walker Manufacturing Company in Indianapolis to FB Ransom, her lawyer, and Alice Kelly, her factory forelady and a former school teacher. Breedloves continues to oversee the business and works in their NYC office. She becomes involved in Harlem's political and social life, including making a $5,000 contribution (equivalent today $72,700) to the NAACP's anti-lynching movement. At the time, this is the largest individual gift to NAACP has ever recieved. Madam CJ Walker Hair Culturists Union of American convention - one of the first national meetings of business women in the country.

"I know how to grow hair as well as I know how to grow cotton. I have built my own factory on my own ground."

Death and Legacy

On May 26, 1919 Breedlove dies of kidney failure and other complications from hypertension at Villa Lewaro, her Irvington-on-Hudson, NY estate. She is 51. She is buried at Woodlawn Cemetary in the Bronx. At the time of her death, her estate's estimated value is $600,000 - $700,000, equivalent of $6 - $7 million in today's dollars. The total sales of compnay during final year of life reached more than $500K, and her combined value of personal assets and business was over $1,000,000. Her will designates that 2⁄3 of future net profits of her estate go to charity.
